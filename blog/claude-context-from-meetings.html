How to give Claude useful context from meetings and messages

A practical system for turning the information scattered through your day into context Claude can reason from later.

Air Note team18 July 20268 min read
On this page What useful context means Capture the right sources Add structure Ask better questions Common mistakes

To give Claude useful context, capture the original details from meetings, voice notes and messages, keep each item connected to its project or person, and ask a specific question that names the outcome you need. Useful context is relevant, structured and traceable to a source.

Claude can help you write, plan and reason, but it cannot automatically know what happened in last week’s meeting or which delivery date your colleague changed. That information lives in your day: in conversations, quick messages, voice notes and decisions that were never written down.

The work is not simply to collect more text. It is to create a useful memory layer between your real work and the question you ask later.

What makes context useful?

Useful context gives Claude enough information to answer accurately without burying the important detail in unrelated material. Four qualities matter most.

  • Relevant: it relates directly to the project, person or decision in the question.
  • Specific: it includes names, dates, numbers and concrete decisions.
  • Structured: summaries, decisions and next steps are easier to use than an undifferentiated transcript.
  • Sourced: you can see which meeting, message or note supports the answer.

More context is not always better. If you provide five long transcripts when the answer depends on one decision, the useful signal becomes harder to find. The aim is a compact, trustworthy set of facts around the question.

Context should reduce the amount of explaining you need to do, not create another document you have to manage.

Capture the sources where work actually happens

Start with the places where information disappears most often. For many people, that means spoken conversations and thoughts captured away from a desk.

Meetings

Capture decisions, objections, dates and ownership. A useful meeting record should tell you what changed and what happens next. The surrounding discussion is helpful only when it explains why a decision was made.

Voice notes

A voice note often contains thinking that has not yet become a task or document. Preserve the idea, then add one sentence explaining where it belongs. “Pricing idea for the annual plan” is more useful later than “thought from the car.”

Messages

Forward or save messages that change the work: a confirmed date, a request, an approval or a new constraint. There is little value in copying an entire conversation if only one message affects the plan.

Your own observations

Facts from a site visit, a customer reaction or a quick idea can matter as much as a formal document. Capture them while the detail is still precise. Air Note lets you send a written thought or voice note through WhatsApp so it does not wait for you to open another system.

Turn raw capture into usable context

Once information is captured, give it enough structure to be useful later. You do not need a complicated filing system. A consistent shape is more important than many categories.

  1. Name the item clearly. Use a title that includes the project, person or topic.
  2. Write a short summary. State what happened in two or three sentences.
  3. Separate facts from actions. Keep decisions, open questions and next steps distinct.
  4. Connect related items. Group the meeting with the message or voice note that followed it.
  5. Preserve the source. Keep the date and origin so an answer can be checked.
A useful context record
  • Project: Savills handover
  • Source: Meeting with Toby, 18 July
  • Decision: Handover moved to the 24th
  • Next step: Send revised floor plans before Friday
  • Constraint: Deposit terms remain unchanged

This structure is small enough to review but complete enough to support a later question. Air Note turns captured meetings, messages and ideas into this kind of context so Claude can use what you already know.

Ask a question that defines the job

A broad prompt such as “tell me about the project” produces a broad answer. A useful question identifies the subject, timeframe and output you need.

Compare these two questions:

Broad: “What happened with delivery?”

Focused: “What did Toby say in last week’s meetings about the delivery deadline, and which actions are due before then?”

The focused version tells Claude where to look and how to shape the answer. You can apply the same pattern to writing and planning tasks:

  • “Draft a follow-up email using the decisions from yesterday’s handover meeting.”
  • “List every open action from my Savills conversations this week, grouped by owner.”
  • “Compare what the client requested in Monday’s meeting with the plan I recorded on Tuesday.”
  • “Create a one-page project update using only confirmed decisions from these sources.”

Common context mistakes

Adding everything without filtering

A large archive is not automatically useful context. Start with the project and timeframe, then include the sources most likely to contain the answer.

Removing dates and sources

A fact without a date may be out of date. A claim without a source is difficult to check. Keep both attached to the captured item.

Mixing decisions with suggestions

“We could move delivery” and “delivery has moved” are not the same. Label confirmed decisions separately from ideas and unresolved questions.

Waiting until the end of the week

Context decays quickly. Capture the meeting, message or voice note when it happens. Review it while names, intent and small details are still fresh.

A good context system feels almost invisible. You capture once, keep the result connected to the work and ask for it when the need appears. The result is not just a better answer from Claude. It is less time spent reconstructing your own week.
